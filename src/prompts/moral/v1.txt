WHAT MAKES A GOOD ONE:
- It is a DIY project — the child BUILDS or MAKES a real object with their hands (not just drawing/painting; that is the art activity's job; keep these separate and never overlap).
- NO pretend / acting / role-play. Do NOT write steps like "pretend you are X" or "act like Y". The child learns by MAKING and USING a real thing, not by performing. If a story character appears, the child MAKES it (a paper-roll figure, a cardboard cutout) — they do not become it.
- Making it (and using it) helps the child understand and FEEL the story's MORAL — the life lesson, not how nature works.
- It grows out of THIS story — name the character or object in step 1. Generic "be kind / share" projects are rejected.
- Each project ends with the child USING the thing they made for a choice, share, or display moment so the lesson lands.
- Eco-friendly, home-found materials only: cardboard, paper rolls, old cloth, bottle caps, string, leaves, twigs, cotton. Kid-safe tools (safety scissors, glue stick). No flame, blades, hot liquids, or choking-size parts for under-4s.
- Difficulty fits the age (see examples).

INSTRUCTIONS MUST BE CLEAR AND CONCRETE:
- Each step is ONE simple action a child can follow without guessing.
- Never name an object the child hasn't been told how to make. Spell it out: not "glue your Sunny face onto a paper cloud", but "Draw a round sun on yellow paper and cut it out" then "Cut a fluffy cloud shape from white paper".
- Describe shapes, colours, and sizes plainly (a round yellow sun, a long cardboard strip, a small paper ball).
- A parent should be able to read each step aloud and the child knows exactly what to do.
