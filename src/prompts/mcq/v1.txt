You write 3 multiple-choice questions a {age}-year-old will shout the

STORY TITLE: {story_title}

DESIGN:
- Q1 — STORY RECALL: a vivid moment from the key moments.
- Q2 — SCIENCE ANCHOR: tests the anchor concept BY NAME, in age-

- Q3 — FEELING / CHOICE: what the character felt or decided at the
  turning point.

EXCITEMENT (do ALL of these — this is what makes a child WANT to play):
- Wrap each question in a tiny playful frame, not a flat "What did X
  do?". Invite the child IN: "Quick — can you guess...", "Uh oh! What
  happened when...", "Be a detective: why did...", "Close your eyes and
  picture it — what...".
- Make Q3 a real CHOICE the child decides ("What would YOU do?" tied to
  the character's turning point), so they feel they are steering it.
- Give at least one question a SURPRISE / "magic" beat — a twist, a
  reveal, or a wow moment the child did not see coming.
- Each fun_fact should land like a little "whoa, really?" reveal, not a
  recap of the answer.

CHALLENGE (must STRETCH the child for age {age}, never trivially easy):
- Age 3-4: simple but ACTIVE — "guess what happens next", sorting,

- Age 5-6: light inference — figure out WHY, predict-then-check.
- Age 7-9: real reasoning, cause-and-effect, or comparing two ideas.
- An answer that is obvious from the question wording scores poorly.
  At least one option should make the child pause and think.

- One short sentence each, 6-12 words.
- Start with What / Why / Who / Where / How / Which — not "Did".
- Use the character's actual name.
- {age}-appropriate vocabulary only.

- Exactly 3 options per question.
- Each option is a complete short answer (2-7 words).
- Wrong options are PLAUSIBLE (things a child might believe),

- All 3 options are roughly the same length.
- The "correct" field is the EXACT text of the correct option.
- Never use "A"/"B"/"C" or "option 1" labels.
